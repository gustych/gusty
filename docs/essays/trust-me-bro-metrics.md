# The number that lies by telling the truth

Around 1902 the French administration in Hanoi set out to rid the city of rats. Plague was coming up through the brand new sewers, the sewers turned out to be a perfect highway for rats, and so the colonial government did the sensible modern thing. It set a target and attached a number to it. A bounty, paid for every rat killed, claimed by handing in a tail.

It worked, by the only measure anyone was watching. Tails came in by the tens of thousands. It also failed completely, because the rats kept multiplying. The historian Michael Vann went through what actually happened. Rat catchers learned to cut the tail off a live rat and let it go, since a rat left breeding produced more tails later. Some people stopped chasing wild rats and simply farmed them. Inspectors began finding tailless rats running around the city. The bounty was honest. The number was real. Every tail handed in was a true tail. And the thing the number was supposed to stand for, a city with fewer rats, moved the wrong way.

Hold that picture, because it is the whole essay.

"Trust me, bro" is the weakest evidence there is, and everyone has learned to laugh at it. So we mostly stopped saying it out loud. We say "here is the number" instead, and the number does the same job better, because a number looks like it has already been checked by something colder and more honest than a person. That is the trick of it. A claim with a figure bolted on feels verified. It usually is not. The figure has verified only itself.

Think about what a metric actually is. Ten thousand users is a true statement and a checkable one. It is a row count, and I can run the query in front of you. What it cannot tell you is the thing you actually wanted to know, which is whether ten thousand real people want the thing enough to come back, or pay, or tell a friend. That fact is not in the database. The number is completely honest about what it is and completely silent about what you hope it means, and we keep mistaking the first for the second. The map is not the territory. A metric is a small, sharp, trustworthy fact about the map, handed to you in the hope that you will treat it as a fact about the territory.

There is a reason this gets worse the moment it matters, and two economists named it. Charles Goodhart, writing about monetary policy in 1975, noticed that a statistical regularity tends to collapse as soon as you start steering by it. Marilyn Strathern later gave it the line everyone quotes: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Donald Campbell said the same thing about social numbers in 1979, that the more you use a quantitative indicator to make decisions, the more it corrupts the very process it was meant to track. The mechanism is not mysterious. A proxy and the real thing sit close together while nobody is looking. Point incentives at the proxy and optimisation flows to the cheapest path that moves the number, and the cheapest path is almost never the one that moves the real thing. The gap between them, harmless a moment ago, blows wide open.

Once you see the shape you see it everywhere. There is the old Soviet joke about the nail factory, judged by the weight of its output, that produced a single enormous nail, and when the rule changed to quantity produced a heap of tiny useless ones. Like the best jokes it was describing something real about planning by target. Wells Fargo told its staff to sell every customer as many products as possible and made the count of accounts the number that mattered, and the staff opened millions of accounts that customers never asked for, because the number said nothing about whether the customer wanted them. Volkswagen built an engine that could detect when it was sitting on the emissions test bench and quietly behave itself, then poured out many times the legal limit of nitrogen oxide the moment it was back on the road. The test passed perfectly. The air did not. Robert McNamara ran a war on the body count, the one number he could collect cleanly, and it told him he was winning a war he was losing.

In every one of those the metric stayed true. Wells Fargo really did open those accounts. The Volkswagen really did pass the test. The bodies were really counted. The number was never the lie. The lie was the silent claim riding underneath it, that the number stood in faithfully for a thing it had quietly stopped touching.

Here is the first principle under all of it. A metric is a compression. You take a situation with a thousand dimensions, a business, a person's health, a war, a community, and you crush it down to one number you can put on a dashboard. Compression like that throws information away, and you cannot get it back out of the number afterwards, because it is gone. This is Shannon's world from the other side. A channel carries a message's bits perfectly and is utterly indifferent to whether the message is true, and a dashboard carries the metric's value perfectly and is utterly indifferent to whether it still means anything. The dishonest move, sometimes against you and very often against yourself, is to present the compressed number as though it were lossless, as though nothing about the real value had been discarded on the way down to one figure.

Which is the same wall this whole series keeps walking into, wearing a lab coat this time. The number lives inside the system, where a check can reach it, and it is genuinely checkable, that is exactly why it is so persuasive. The value it is supposed to represent lives outside the system, in the world, where no check can reach. A "trust me bro" metric is what you get when someone takes a fact from the wrong side of that line and dresses it in the clothing of a fact from the right side. It is the well formed lie from the data contracts, scaled up from one bad row to an entire pitch deck. It is precisely the failure the rest of these essays keep circling, pretending the line is not there. Goodhart's law is that wall, and the cobra farms and the tailless rats are what the wall looks like when you ignore it.

So what do you actually do, if every metric is a proxy and every proxy can be gamed. You do not go hunting for the perfect ungameable number, because there is no such thing, and the search for one is its own kind of self deception. You do two honest things instead. You say out loud what the number does not capture, which side of the line it is sitting on, so nobody including you mistakes the map for the ground. And you staple a refuter to it. You name, in advance and in public, the real world outcome that would prove the number is lying to you, and then you watch for it.

I will do it here with my own. OpenLEG ranks Swiss towns by untapped rooftop solar and sends me at the best scores first. That ranking is a metric, and it has exactly this disease waiting in it. The danger is not that the number is wrong, the roofs are really there. The danger is that I start believing the ranking is the readiness of a community, when readiness, whether neighbours will actually trust each other and show up, lives on the far side of the line where no ranking can follow. So here is the refuter, in the open where you can hold me to it. If the towns my model scores low organise themselves anyway, while the ones it loves sit quiet, then my number was measuring the wrong thing, and the ranking belongs in the bin no matter how clean it looks. A metric with its own refutation stapled to it is the only honest kind. Everything else is "trust me, bro" with a number where the bro used to be.

And the claim this rests on, with the way to break it. Every metric of something genuinely human, health, learning, trust, the worth of a thing you made, is a proxy with an irreducible gap between the number and the thing, and that gap can always be prised open by moving the number without moving the thing. To prove me wrong, show me one metric of a real human value that cannot be gamed, where pushing the figure up can never come apart from improving the thing it stands for, where the map is provably the territory. I do not think it exists, because the number lives inside the system and the value lives outside it, on the far side of the same wall I keep finding. Until someone hands me that number, I am going to keep saying which side of the line mine are on, and telling you what would prove them false.
