# Why a scratched disc still plays

You cn prbbly rd ths sntnce evn wth hlf th lttrs gone. That is not a party trick. It is the most useful fact about communication anyone has written down, and a quiet engineer at Bell Labs turned it into mathematics in 1948.

Claude Shannon's paper, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," did something that still sounds impossible when you say it in plain words. It proved you can send a message reliably over a channel that is actively corrupting it. Not by fixing the channel. By changing the message.

First he had to say what information even was, which nobody had pinned down before. Shannon measured it as surprise. A message carries more information the less predictable it is, and he gave that quantity a unit, the bit, and a measure, entropy. Then he turned to the channel, the wire or the air or the disc, the thing in the middle that adds noise and flips your bits whether you like it or not.

Here is the result that made his name. Every channel has a number attached to it, a capacity, the most information it can carry per second. Shannon proved that as long as you transmit below that capacity, you can push the error rate as close to zero as you want, across a channel that is corrupting you the whole time. Reliability that is essentially perfect, over hardware that is essentially unreliable. People did not believe it at first, because it felt like getting something for nothing.

The trick is that you do not get it for nothing. You pay in redundancy. You stop sending the bare message and start sending a coded version with structure deliberately added, so that even after the noise has chewed on it, only one original could have produced what arrived. The reliability is built into the message, not into the wire. The message carries its own repair kit.

A few years later Richard Hamming, also at Bell Labs, built the first real codes that did this, with extra parity bits arranged so that a single flipped bit not only announced itself but pointed to exactly which bit had flipped, so the receiver could fix it without asking for a resend. The idea grew up fast. Reed-Solomon codes, from 1960, are the reason a scratched CD still plays, a smudged QR code still scans, and a probe at the edge of the solar system can whisper a picture back across billions of kilometres through nothing but noise and have it arrive clean. In every case the disc or the code or the transmission was built with enough redundancy to survive losing a chunk of itself.

This is the purest version of the move the whole series is about. Every other essay is about learning not to depend on a person or an institution you have no reason to trust. Shannon is about not depending on a channel you have every reason to distrust, because physics guarantees it will corrupt you. His answer is the cleanest one anyone has found. Do not trust the channel. Build the reliability into the message itself, as structure you can check, and spend redundancy to buy it. It is the tally stick again, in a way. The split half of the stick was a redundant copy that let you catch tampering, a checksum in wood, and a parity bit is the same instinct with the maths filled in. It is also the gentler cousin of the Byzantine generals, who had to agree on a plan while some of them were sending corrupted messages on purpose. Shannon's noise is not malicious, just indifferent, but the shape of the fix is the same. Add enough honest structure and the corruption cannot hide.

And then, right at the start of the paper, Shannon draws the line this series keeps running into, and he draws it more sharply than anyone before or since. He writes that the messages frequently have meaning, that they refer to real things in the world, and then he sets that aside in one sentence. "These semantic aspects of communication are irrelevant to the engineering problem." He is not being careless. He is being exact. His theory protects and recovers the structure of a message through any amount of noise, and it says nothing whatsoever about whether the message is true, or what it means, or whether it matters.

That is the boundary, stated by the man who built the field, in 1948. You can mechanise the survival of a message's form. You cannot mechanise its truth. A picture from Voyager can arrive bit-perfect after a billion kilometres of noise and still show you something you misread. A lie travels through an error-correcting code exactly as faithfully as the truth, because the code is counting structure, not checking the world. This is the same wall the smart contract hits at the oracle, the same wall the data contract hits when a number is well formed and quietly wrong. Shannon just got there first, and named it.

There are honest limits even on the structural side. Below the channel's capacity you can drive error to nearly zero, but above it no code in the world will save you. Push more information than the channel can hold and it degrades, no matter how cleverly you encode. Shannon told you the price as precisely as he told you the prize.

So here is the claim, and the way to break it. You can recover a message's structure through noise by mechanical means alone, and you can never, by those same means, recover whether it is true. To prove me wrong, show me a coding scheme that flags a message as false, not malformed but a well-formed lie, using only the mathematics of the channel and nothing from outside it. I do not think it exists, because truth lives outside the channel, on the far side of the exact line Shannon drew. Everything inside the line we have learned to protect beautifully. Everything outside it is still ours to worry about, which is, I think, where it belongs.
