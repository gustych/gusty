# A contract between teams who never talk

It usually breaks at the worst possible time. A dashboard the whole company reads on Monday morning shows a zero, or worse, shows a number that looks plausible and is quietly wrong. Someone traces it back through a chain of pipelines and models and finds the cause three teams away. An engineer renamed a column last Tuesday, or changed what "active user" meant, and had no idea anyone downstream was leaning on the old version. By their own lights they did nothing wrong. They never agreed to keep it stable. The breakage is not a bug in the code. It is a bug in the trust between two teams who have never spoken.

Data contracts are the current answer to that. The idea grew out of Zhamak Dehghani's data mesh in 2019, which argued for handing ownership of data back to the teams that produce it. It got its name and shape from Andrew Jones at GoCardless around the end of 2021, then spread fast through Chad Sanderson's writing, a small industry of tools, and an open specification. A data contract is an agreement between the team that produces a dataset and the teams that consume it, about the shape of what flows between them: the schema, the types, which fields can be null, what range a number is allowed to take, what the fields actually mean, who owns it, and how fresh and complete it will arrive.

Written out like that, it sounds like paperwork. The part that makes it a contract rather than a memo is enforcement. The producer's commitment gets checked automatically, at the source, before the data ships. A schema registry refuses an incompatible change. A test suite fails the pull request. The check runs in the pipeline every time, and nobody has to remember to care.

This is the same move the whole series keeps circling. You take an assumption that used to live in a colleague's head and their goodwill, you narrow it down to a fact a machine can verify, and you build the verification into the structure, so trust is no longer needed where a check will do. It is the tally stick, really. The producer and the consumer each hold the same agreed schema, two halves of a split stick, and the contract holds only as long as the producer's output still matches the half the consumer is holding. A mismatch is the broken stick, caught the moment it happens instead of weeks later in a meeting about why the numbers look off. It is the Roman stipulatio too, in the sense that the check is literal and unforgiving. A field that should be an integer and arrives as a string fails, whatever anyone meant by it, because the form is the thing being tested.

Then you hit the wall this series always hits.

A data contract can mechanise structure and nothing more. It can guarantee that a field is a non-null number inside a sane range and arrives by six in the morning. It cannot guarantee that the number is correct. It cannot tell you whether the figure reflects anything real about the world, and it will not notice when "revenue" quietly starts meaning something different than it meant last quarter while every type still checks out. Structure lives inside the data, where a deterministic check can reach it. Truth and meaning live outside, where it cannot. This is the oracle problem again in analytics clothes. A closed system can confirm that a message is well formed and stay completely blind to whether it is true.

There is a law that names why the contract is needed and, in the same breath, why it can never fully win. Hyrum's law says that with enough users of an interface, every observable behaviour of that interface ends up depended on by somebody, no matter what the contract promises. Some downstream team is relying on a quirk you never promised and never thought to write down. So the contract can never list everything that will actually break, and the same fact that forces you to write contracts guarantees they will always be incomplete.

There is even an old argument about how strict to be. Postel's robustness principle told us for decades to be conservative in what we send and liberal in what we accept, and a generation of systems were built to quietly swallow malformed input out of politeness. The modern reversal, written down by the IETF in 2023, is that all that leniency just lets the rot accumulate until nobody knows what the real shape is anymore. Data contracts come down hard on the strict side. Reject the broken thing at the source, loudly, rather than absorb it and pass the confusion downstream.

The honest part is where it fails, and it does not fail on the technology. A data contract only helps if the upstream team agrees to be bound by it and keeps caring once the tooling stops being new. That is not an engineering problem. It is a reputation problem, the exact weakness the medieval merchant law had with outsiders. The system works beautifully among people who share an incentive to be trusted, and falls apart against anyone who has decided not to play. A schema registry can make a breakage visible and stamp someone's name on it, the way Certificate Transparency turned silent misissuance into a public log entry. It cannot manufacture the will to honour the promise. It moves the breakage upstream and into the open, which is real progress, and it stops exactly there.

So data contracts are not a new kind of thing. They are the same thing again, the newest layer on a five thousand year old habit. They take one checkable slice of a relationship and make it mechanical, and they leave the human part, whether this team will keep its word, honestly where it has always lived.

Here is the claim, and the way to break it. A data contract catches malformed data, never wrong data. If you can show me one that caught a number that was perfectly well formed and quietly false, meaning drifted while every type still passed, then the line I am drawing between structure and truth is in the wrong place, and structure was never the real limit. I do not think that contract exists, because the check lives inside the data and the truth it is meant to protect lives outside it.
